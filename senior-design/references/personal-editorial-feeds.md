# Personal Editorial Feeds

Use this for personal sites, founder/designer portfolios, update feeds, journals, writing indexes, lightweight blogs, and pages where the person is the product signal.

## Design Thesis

Make the page feel authored, not templated. The strongest move is usually text, pacing, and identity, not decoration.

## Structural Lessons

- Keep navigation small, plain, and identity-led: name, role, a few links.
- Let the current section feel selected through weight or one tiny accent, not a full nav treatment.
- Use one large editorial headline to establish voice, then get out of the way.
- Treat the body as a chronological feed with clear entries, not a card grid.
- Use metadata chips for date, location, category, or status.
- Put generous vertical air before the first entry; the silence is part of the tone.
- Let images be occasional interruptions, not mandatory thumbnails for every post.
- Prefer a narrow or offset content column over full-width layout.

## Typography

- Pair a high-character display face with a quiet sans-serif body.
- Serif or editorial display type can be large here, but keep it intentional and sparse.
- Body copy should feel readable and conversational: `17-22px`, relaxed line height, normal weight.
- Avoid heavy weights. Most text should sit around `400-520`.
- Use punctuation and line breaks as composition tools.

## Color And Material

- Default to white or warm off-white.
- Use black/graphite text with very light gray secondary text.
- One tiny accent is enough: link marker, selected nav mark, chip icon, or cursor-like detail.
- Avoid gradients, glass, shadows, panels, and dark SaaS atmosphere.

## Feed Rhythm

- Entry metadata should scan before the paragraph.
- Alternate long and short entries naturally; do not force equal heights.
- Use lists only when the content calls for them.
- Links can live inline; do not over-style them.
- Keep dividers subtle or absent. Spacing can separate entries.

## Good For

- Designer/founder personal websites.
- Advisory or consultancy update logs.
- Personal changelogs.
- Writing feeds where personality matters.
- Portfolio pages that should feel human and current.

## Avoid

- Turning every update into a card.
- Adding badges, metrics, and fake product proof.
- Using centered SaaS hero + CTA patterns.
- Over-designing the feed with timelines, sticky sidebars, or animated clutter.
- Copying someone’s personal content, name treatment, or exact headline structure.

## Quality Check

- Does the page feel like a real person wrote it?
- Can the reader understand who this is in five seconds?
- Is the first headline memorable without shouting?
- Does the feed invite reading instead of scanning tiles?
- Would the page still work if all decoration was removed?
