# Premium Dark SaaS Pattern

Use this when designing high-end SaaS, AI products, creator tools, operating systems, studios, or dark product landing pages.

## Core Feel

Aim for a premium product launch page, not a generic dark template.

- Black or near-black foundation.
- White editorial headlines with one vivid accent word or phrase.
- Restrained sans-serif UI chrome.
- Serif or refined display type for big product promises.
- A single electric accent, usually blue, violet-blue, cyan, or controlled red-orange.
- Wide, calm spacing with dense detail only inside product visuals.
- Product-native abstract visuals: particle fields, node maps, canvases, timelines, layers, graphs, command surfaces.

Avoid glows, random gradients, floating blobs, and decorative dashboards that do not explain the product.

## Page Architecture

Use a complete landing-page rhythm:

1. Header: logo, 4-6 nav items, log in, primary CTA.
2. Hero: strong product promise, short supporting copy, two CTAs, large abstract/product visual.
3. Trust row: restrained logo strip or short proof line.
4. System section: sharp headline plus 3-4 capability cards.
5. Proof or scale section: one concise claim plus 2-3 metrics or operating benefits.
6. Conversion band: bold accent block with one CTA.
7. Footer: logo, short description, grouped links.

Do not repeat centered feature sections. Vary scale and density.

## Hero Rules

- Keep the first viewport readable; do not create giant empty gaps.
- Put copy on the left and visual energy on the right only when the visual meaningfully supports the promise.
- Use a compact headline scale: large enough to lead, not so large it becomes poster art.
- Make one word carry the accent color when it sharpens positioning.
- Keep supporting copy specific and under 3 short lines on desktop when possible.
- Make the primary CTA visually decisive; make the secondary CTA quiet.

## Abstract Visuals

Abstract visuals must feel tied to the product.

Good motifs:

- Particle mesh representing references, knowledge, taste, planning, or systems.
- Layer stacks for export, publishing, or production.
- Canvas controls for creative tools.
- Node paths for workflows or collaboration.
- Small colored signal points for attention, events, or ideas.

Rules:

- Use one visual language across the page.
- Let visuals sit in the page, not inside random decorative wrappers.
- Keep UI text code-native unless text belongs inside an illustrative asset.
- If using CSS/SVG/canvas particles, keep them subtle and performant.

## Cards

Use cards only when they frame distinct capabilities.

- Dark surface slightly lighter than the page.
- Thin border with low contrast.
- Small accent label at top.
- Short serif heading.
- Practical body copy.
- Visual motif anchored to the lower area.
- No generic icon grid.

Each card should answer: what capability does this product unlock?

## Copy Posture

Write like a serious product, not a hype page.

- Lead with what the product changes.
- Use short claims with concrete nouns.
- Avoid "unlock", "revolutionary", "seamless", "supercharge", and vague AI filler.
- Keep feature copy operational: collect, plan, refine, build, export, share, align.
- Use proof rows and metrics only when they support credibility.

## Visual QA

Before finishing, check:

- Does the page feel premium without relying on glow effects?
- Is the accent color used with discipline?
- Are feature cards distinct but clearly part of one system?
- Is the hero visual meaningful, not just pretty?
- Does the first viewport have enough content without dead air?
- Do headings fit comfortably on desktop and mobile?
- Are dark-mode contrast and focus states usable?
