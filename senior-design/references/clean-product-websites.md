# Clean Product Websites

Use this for simple SaaS/product websites, developer tools, AI tools, infrastructure products, and landing pages where the goal is clarity, trust, and product taste.

## Reference Set

Study Cursor, Vercel, and Linear for structure. Do not copy their brand, copy, logo, exact layouts, or visuals.

## Core Feel

The page should feel useful before it feels impressive.

- Neutral foundation: white, near-white, black, or near-black.
- Small, calm navigation.
- Clear product promise in plain language.
- One primary CTA, one quiet secondary action.
- Product screenshot, app chrome, code surface, or workflow preview as proof.
- Sparse color, used for action, status, data, or brand signal.
- Generous whitespace around marketing copy; dense detail only inside product visuals.

## Page Model

Default structure:

1. Header: logo, 4-6 links, account action, primary CTA.
2. Hero: clear headline, short support copy, 1-2 CTAs.
3. Product proof: real interface, workflow, code, terminal, dashboard, or canvas.
4. Trust/proof: logos, metrics, customer quote, or credible usage detail.
5. Capability sections: fewer, deeper sections with product visuals.
6. Conversion close: simple CTA, not a second hero.

Avoid stacking generic feature cards when a product surface would explain the product better.

## Typography

- Use clean sans-serif by default.
- Keep most font weights between `400-560`.
- Reserve `600-650` for buttons, active nav, or small labels.
- Avoid `700+` except for rare brand/editorial moments.
- Desktop hero headline: usually `40-64px`.
- Desktop section headline: usually `28-44px`.
- Mobile hero headline: usually `30-38px`.
- Letter spacing should be normal or very slightly tight; never crushed.
- Body copy should be medium gray, specific, and short.

## Visuals

Product-native visuals beat abstract decoration.

Good:

- Real dashboard chrome.
- Browser window with credible app content.
- Code editor/terminal/agent transcript for developer tools.
- Timeline, issue list, inspector, deploy flow, or workflow state.
- Subtle grid or construction lines when they support technical clarity.

Avoid:

- Random blobs, orbs, aurora fields, and decorative gradients.
- Fake metric cards that do not connect to a workflow.
- Glass panels for their own sake.
- Stock-like abstract art.
- Hero visuals that cannot explain what the product does.

## Composition

- Keep the first viewport calm and legible.
- Put spectacle below or behind the product proof, not on top of it.
- Use one strong visual anchor; do not scatter many small decorative pieces.
- Let product surfaces sit in a stable container with believable scale.
- Leave room around the hero so it feels confident, not crowded.
- Show the next section just enough to imply depth.

## Copy

Write like a product team, not a pitch deck.

- Prefer concrete nouns: deploys, issues, reviews, agents, previews, workflows.
- Say what changes for the user.
- Avoid hype words: revolutionary, unlock, unleash, supercharge, seamless.
- Use labels that describe actual product states.

## QA

Before shipping:

- Can someone understand the product in 3 seconds?
- Is the product proof more informative than the decoration?
- Are font weights calm?
- Does the hero avoid giant headline behavior?
- Does the page still work if all decorative backgrounds are removed?
